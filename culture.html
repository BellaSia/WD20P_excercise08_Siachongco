Tribe of Ifugao

Tribe of Ifugao The famed rice terraces were erected almost 2,000 years ago by the Ifugao Tribe of Baguio City. Ifugao descendants use colorful woven fabrics and headdresses in traditional garb. Metalworking in iron and brass, weaving, and animal sacrifice are cultural characteristics shared by all Igorot peoples. They believe in spirits, especially ancestors, and have elaborate ceremonies to appease them.

Panagbenga FLower Festival

Every fourth week of February, the Panagbenga Flower Festival is held as a thanksgiving ceremony for Mother Nature. Local tribes dance in native clothes as colorful floats parade along the streets. The Microtel Inn is a great place to see the parades from. The festivals, events, and attractions all represent the culture of Baguio in some manner. The residents enjoy a reasonably good level of living and are quite well-off, as well as being welcoming to tourists.

Igorot Native Languages

Ibaloi, Kankana-ey, and Ifugao, as well as Ilocano, Pangasinan, and Kapampangan, are extensively spoken in Baguio. Many people in and around the city understand both Filipino and English. Several restaurants also provide a variety of local and international cuisines. A place's culture and tradition are reflections of its society. Baguio's culture and traditions are unique and captivating. It teaches us about the people who live there, their way of life, and their values. Observing Baguio culture provides you a good sense of the customs and practices that the inhabitants still observe.

Igorot Music instrument

Gongs, which come in a variety of sizes and metal components, are usually banged by males to give rhythm and melody for the dances. Every province in the Northern Philippines' upland area has its own pace, rhythm, or musical interpretation. The pounding is also determined by the reason for which the gongs are brought out and played. The sound of gongs during a feast also represents nobility and thankfulness for major events such as weddings, family reunions, healing of the sick, or even the closing rituals of a burial or acknowledgement of an important person in the village. Metalworking in iron and brass, weaving, and animal sacrifice are cultural characteristics shared by all Igorot peoples. They believe in spirits, especially ancestors, and have elaborate ceremonies to appease them. The Gong would be passed from one province to the next, completing a circle around the region. It would culminate in its return to the host province on the Cordillera's 150th anniversary.
